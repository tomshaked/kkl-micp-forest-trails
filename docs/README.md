# Research Proposal

Full research proposal submitted to KKL-JNF under Call Target 6.5.5 — Stabilization of Forest Roads and Trails.

---

## Background and Problem

Forest roads, cycling paths, and hiking trails managed by KKL-JNF are built on compacted soil, stabilized aggregates, or rigid surfaces (asphalt, concrete). Each has significant drawbacks:

- **Compacted soil** — erodes quickly, requires repeated maintenance, destabilizes after rain
- **Stabilized aggregates** — deteriorate under use and runoff over short cycles
- **Rigid surfaces** — durable but high carbon footprint, damage the forest's natural character, and create artificial environments

KKL-JNF faces a dual challenge: high maintenance costs and environmental impact from conventional materials.

---

## Proposed Solution

**Microbially Induced Calcite Precipitation (MICP)** is a biological process in which ureolytic bacteria produce urease enzymes that generate carbonate ions. These react with calcium ions to form calcite crystals within soil pores, binding particles and improving mechanical strength, water resistance, and erosion resistance.

Key advantages over cement and asphalt:
- Uses local resources and indigenous microorganisms
- Reduces carbon emissions
- Preserves the ecological character of forest trails

The research combines MICP with **robotic application** for:
1. Uniform and controlled spraying of MICP solutions at scale
2. 3D printing of trail surfaces optimized for different path types
3. 3D printing of integrated trail elements (seating, rest stops, shade structures) from locally stabilized soil

---

## Research Questions and Hypotheses

**Research question:** Can MICP based on indigenous soil bacteria be applied via robotic tools to stabilize forest trails in a durable, environmentally friendly, and more efficient manner than existing methods?

**Working hypotheses:**
- Indigenous bacteria will enable effective MICP in diverse Israeli forest soils without external bacterial inputs
- Robotic application will improve process uniformity, reduce labor dependency, and enable design freedom
- MICP-stabilized trails will outperform existing methods on cracking, erosion, vegetation regrowth, and maintenance frequency

---

## Research Objectives

- Develop MICP-treated soil mixtures adapted to soil types found in Israeli forest trails
- Evaluate mechanical strength, cracking resistance, water permeability, and vegetation growth vs. existing methods (ASTM D2166, ASTM D5084)
- Develop robotic application protocols for spraying and 3D printing
- Assess environmental and economic contributions relative to conventional alternatives

---

## Prior Art and Knowledge Gaps

**Soil stabilization** — conventional methods (cement, lime, asphalt) are effective but environmentally costly (Afrin, 2017; Firoozi et al., 2017).

**MICP** — demonstrated at lab and field scale to improve strength, reduce permeability, and limit erosion (Mujah et al., 2017; van Paassen et al., 2010). Studies in Israel confirmed biostimulation of local ureolytic bacteria is feasible without external bacterial culture (Gat et al., 2016; Gomez et al., 2018; Raveh-Amit et al., 2024).

**Robotic earth printing** — demonstrated for architectural applications using local soil as feedstock (Bar-Sinai et al., 2021; Asaf et al., 2023). Integration of MICP into printed structures shown in preliminary work (Hillel et al., 2023, 2024).

**Gap:** No prior work combines MICP with robotics for forest trail infrastructure.

---

## Preliminary Results

### MICP Field and Lab Study
Raveh-Amit et al. (2024) stabilized Negev loess soils via biostimulation of indigenous bacteria. Key findings:
- Confirmed calcite precipitation (pH rise, urea concentration drop)
- Reduced crack formation over multiple treatment cycles
- Significant reduction in wind erosion in tunnel tests

### Robotic Earth Printing
Bar-Sinai et al. (2021) and Asaf et al. (2023) developed robotic workflows for local-soil 3D printing, including material characterization and computational design methods.

### MICP + Robotics Integration
Hillel et al. (2023, 2024) demonstrated 3D printing of continuous shell structures from biostimulated soil. MICP improved structural performance and enabled larger-scale fabrication, with confirmed calcite precipitation in printed and cast specimens.

## Expected Impact for KKL-JNF

- Reduced trail maintenance costs and repair frequency
- Elimination or reduction of cement and asphalt in managed forest areas
- Preservation of forest ecological character and visitor experience
- Positioning KKL-JNF as a leader in nature-based infrastructure innovation
