## Abstract

KKL-JNF faces continuous challenges in maintaining forest roads, cycling paths, and hiking trails in forests and open landscapes. These paths are usually constructed from compacted soil, stabilized aggregates, or rigid surfaces such as asphalt and concrete. Each approach has drawbacks: natural soil wears quickly and requires frequent maintenance, stabilized aggregates deteriorate after rainfall and heavy use, and rigid surfaces, while durable, damage the natural character of the forest, have a high carbon footprint, and create artificial surfaces in natural landscapes.

This research proposes an environmentally friendly and scientifically grounded alternative: **Microbially Induced Calcite Precipitation (MICP)**. This natural process relies on indigenous soil bacteria that induce calcite precipitation, binding soil particles and strengthening the ground. Unlike cement and asphalt, this method uses local resources, reduces carbon emissions, and minimizes the ecological footprint of trail infrastructure.

The novelty of the project lies in combining MICP with **robotic application**. Robotics allow precise and uniform deployment, scalability, and reduced reliance on manual labor. In addition, robotic tools will enable **3D printing** of integrated elements such as benches, resting areas, and shading structures — all fabricated from stabilized local soil.

## Research Phases

### Phase 1 — Laboratory Experiments (Months 1–9)
Development and testing of soil-MICP mixtures, including:
- Strength (Unconfined Compressive Strength, ASTM D2166)
- Cracking resistance
- Water permeability (ASTM D5084)
- Vegetation growth

### Phase 2 — Robotic Application (Months 7–18)
Development of controlled spraying and 3D printing processes using a KUKA KR60 industrial robotic arm:
- Uniform spraying protocols for MICP solutions
- 3D printing of trail surfaces and integrated furniture elements
- Laboratory validation and reproducibility testing

### Phase 3 — Field Test (Months 16–24)
Implementation and monitoring at a selected KKL-JNF forest trail (~10 m segment):
- Periodic monitoring of cracking, erosion, vegetation growth, and soil displacement
- Comparison with a control segment on the same trail

### Phase 4 — Analysis and Reporting (Months 9–12 & 22–24)
- Data analysis and summary reports submitted to KKL-JNF

### Phase 5 — Knowledge Sharing and IP (Months 9–12 & 22–24)
- Publication of peer-reviewed articles (Q1 journals)
- Presentation at professional conferences
- Patent filings for developed innovations

## Preliminary Results

### MICP Soil Stabilization — Lab and Field
A preliminary study (Raveh-Amit et al., 2024) demonstrated that loess soils in the Negev can be stabilized via biostimulation of indigenous ureolytic bacteria. Results showed:
- Successful calcite precipitation confirmed by pH rise and urea concentration drop
- Reduced crack formation after multiple treatment cycles
- Significant decrease in dust emission in wind tunnel tests

### Robotic Earth Printing
Previous collaborations (Bar-Sinai et al., 2021; Asaf et al., 2023) demonstrated the feasibility of using local soil as a feedstock for robotic 3D printing, establishing workflows for computational design, material characterization, and fabrication.

### MICP + Robotics Integration
Hillel et al. (2023, 2024) demonstrated 3D printing of continuous shell structures from biostimulated soil, showing that MICP improves structural performance and enables larger-scale fabrication. Successful calcite precipitation was confirmed within printed and cast specimens.
